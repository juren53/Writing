# Mr. Truman's Music
## How Music Shaped the Man Who Would Become the 33rd President

**Author:** Jim U'Ren
**Status:** In development — March 2026

---

## Premise

Many books have been written about Harry S. Truman. This one tells the story through a different lens: the role music played across every phase of his life — boyhood, hardship, public service, and retirement — and how that lifelong passion helped mold the man who became America's 33rd president. Like many musicians, Harry was his own worst critic. That tension between his private musical life and his public self-assessment is one of the book's central threads.

---

## Structure Overview

The book follows Truman's life chronologically, using music as the connective tissue across five broad movements:

1. **The Making** — boyhood, the trolley rides, Mrs. White, and the abrupt end
2. **The Interlude** — farm years, the war, Kansas City's jazz world, the Senator
3. **The Ascent** — Vice President, the piano at the National Press Club, Benny
4. **The Presidency** — the White House Steinway, Margaret's career, Key West
5. **The Legacy** — what music gave him, the record collection, the man in full

#### Chapter 2 — Young Man on a Mission
**Working title:** *The Ride on the Winner Line*
**File:** `Book/chapters/young-mr-truman.doc` ✓ **Canonical draft exists**

A young Harry hurries down the sidewalk with his music tucked under his arm. Twice a week, from age 10, he catches the Winner Electric Trolley from Independence to Kansas City to study with Grace White at 27th and Brooklyn. The trolley ride itself is transformative — from Antebellum Independence ("Southern town") into "Yankee Town" Kansas City. At Mrs. White's, Harry plays a Steinway concert grand — a world apart from the Kimball upright at home. Harry is considered her best student.

**Key themes:** The journey as metaphor; bridging North and South; music as window to a larger world

#### Chapter 3 — Mrs. White and the World Beyond
**Working title:** *A Lesson in More Than Music*
**File:** `Book/chapters/ch03-mrs-white.md` *(to be drafted)*

Grace White was no ordinary piano teacher. Trained in Vienna by Theodore Leschetizky — teacher of Paderewski and scores of the era's greatest pianists — she carried a direct lineage from the European classical tradition into a parlor on the eastern edge of Kansas City. A piano lesson with Mrs. White was more than piano: it was history, culture, Europe, and the world beyond Missouri. These connections Harry would carry for the rest of his life.

**Key themes:** The Leschetizky lineage; music as cultural education; Europe arriving in Independence
**Source materials:**
- `Research/mrs-white.md` — biographical notes (born 1860 Jackson MS; died 1937 KC; husband Edwin C. White 1830–1907)

#### Chapter 4 — The End of Lessons
**Working title:** *Family First*
**File:** `Book/chapters/end-of-lessons.md` ✓ **Draft fragment exists**

In 1901, John Truman's business fortunes collapsed. The family liquidated assets in Independence and moved back to the Grandview farm. Harry, now 17, was needed. He could not continue his lessons with Mrs. White — to do so would have meant turning his back on his family, and that was never in the cards. Later in life Harry would rationalize the decision with "I wasn't good enough" and "piano playing was for sissies" — self-deprecations that rang hollow to those who knew him. He had learned a lesson that would define his character: family and friends come first.

**Key themes:** Sacrifice; self-deprecation as shield; music as private life; character formation
**Source materials:**
- `Book/chapters/end-of-lessons.md` — draft fragment
- HSTL biographical materials on the Grandview years (to be sourced)

**Research gaps:** Exact date lessons ended; whether Harry ever visited Mrs. White after 1901; John Truman's specific business failure; Harry's own words on ending lessons (diary, letters)

#### Chapter 8 — "Mr. Vice President, Would You Hold My Cigar?"
**Working title:** *Truman and Benny at the National Press Club*
**File:** `Book/chapters/ch08-npc-benny.md` *(to be drafted)*

January 1945. Harry S. Truman has been Vice President for less than two weeks. At the inaugural program for NPC President Jamieson, Jack Benny — perennial funnyman — hands his cigar to the VP and sits down at the Club's piano. The beginning of a long friendship. The moment captures something essential about Truman: music as common ground, as social connector, as equalizer of rank.

**Key themes:** Music as social currency; the democratic nature of a piano bench; VP to President in 83 days
