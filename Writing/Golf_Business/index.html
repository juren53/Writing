The Public Golf Course Business

An op-ed on public assets and public accountability in Platte County

When the Platte County Commission voted to sell Shiloh Springs Golf Course to a private golf chain in 2018, they offered a simple justification: the course couldn't pay for itself. The public was told this was a practical decision, not a political one. The numbers, they said, just didn't add up.

The numbers did add up — just not on the commission's timeline.

Within a single year of the sale, the course was operating in the black. Today it is reportedly the most profitable course in the entire private chain that purchased it. Whatever management problems existed under public ownership, the new owners apparently found them quite solvable.

This outcome should surprise no one familiar with the economics of municipal golf. Public golf courses across the country routinely cover their operating costs and return revenue to the parks and recreation departments they anchor. They provide accessible, affordable outdoor recreation — a genuine public good — while paying their own way. The premise that a golf course is an inherent financial burden on taxpayers is simply not borne out by public courses across the country.

To be fair, there is one thing to be grateful for: the course is still a golf course. It was not carved up by a developer. Green space was preserved.

But that green space is now preserved only at the pleasure of its private owners. The public interest in that land — the interest that owned the course, maintained it, and kept it open to all — was surrendered the day the Platte County Commission approved the sale. What happens to it next is no longer a public decision.

The golf course sale is not an isolated incident. More recently, Platte County voters approved a tax projected to raise roughly $5 million annually for children's mental health services. The public had spoken clearly. One month later, the Platte County Commission voted unanimously to refuse to enact it. Not one dissenting vote. The will of the voters was set aside as if the election had never happened. And now an appeals court recently affirmed that the Commission legally blocked a voter-approved quarter-cent sales tax for a children's services fund.

These two episodes — a public asset quietly sold off, a voter-approved tax simply ignored — point to something larger than any single decision. They suggest a commission that has grown comfortable substituting its own judgment for the public's, without consequence.

Elected officials make consequential choices. When those choices result in the transfer of a profitable public asset to private hands, or the nullification of a democratic vote, the public deserves more than silence. They deserve an accounting.

The lesson here is not complicated: hold your elected officials accountable. Ask hard questions before public assets are sold. And remember that "it can't pay for itself" is a claim, not a fact — and one that, in this case, turned out to be spectacularly wrong.
